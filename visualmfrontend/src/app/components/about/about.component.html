The Eco Materials Archive is an open-source label generator and collaborative archive for documenting and sharing material experiments. It was built around a simple idea: that creative communities — especially students — should be able to build a shared living archive of alternative design materials together, both online and on the walls of their studios and labs.


The library focuses on materials that are easily renewable, recyclable, home-compostable within 90 days, locally sourced, or derived from local waste streams.


What can you do with it?

• Generate a printable PDF label in A6, A5, or A4 format to attach to your sample.

• Save drafts of material recipes you made or discovered.

• You can submit material recipes to be published on the online repository after the admin approval.

• Browse samples made by peers, physically in the lab or online.


Can't find your institution?

Are you a student trying to share your recipes, but you can't find your institution? That's probably because your institute is not in our database yet. Let your teacher contact us, and we'll set them up with an admin account.


How do I add my institution?

Do you want to use the Eco Materials Archive in your education, and let your students upload their DIY material recipes as well? You can! Contact Sam Edens at s.j.edens@hva.nl to request an admin account. Once you receive a private URL to access the admin panel, you can set the logo and name of your institution and approve the work your students are uploading.


Who made this?


The Eco Materials Archive was developed by the Fashion Research and Technology group and the Biomaterials Studio at the Amsterdam University of Applied Sciences, together with the Eco Materials Libraries at the University of California Davis and University of Colorado Boulder: Sam Edens, Eldy Lazaro Vasquez, Francesco Sollitto, Troy Nachtigall, and Maarten Groen. The script is available via this GitHub repository.


It is a continuation of the Material Sample Management Tool, originally developed in 2020 at AUAS by computer engineering students Alec Wouda, Sam Overheul, Kostas Mylothridis, Mitchell de Vries, and Jarno van der Velde — with guidance from Okechukwu Onwunli and the Enterprise Web Applications teaching team — under the supervision of senior lecturer and researcher Loes Bogers.


That original tool was part of a broader material archiving project funded by NWO through a Comenius Teaching Fellowship awarded to Loes Bogers, developed in close collaboration with Textile Lab Amsterdam at Waag. It drew inspiration from the Material Archive created at Waag by Cecilia Raspanti, Maria Viftrup, and others (2017–2019), and ran until January 2022.


The project was further supported by the AUAS Learning Community on Critical Making and Research through Design — a partnership between the Amsterdam Fashion Institute and the research groups Fashion Research & Technology, Visual Methodologies, and Collective Play and Civic Media.

FAQ


What are the eco compatibility tags? How do you define them?

• Made of renewable resources means that the resources used take less than a human lifetime to replenish, but preferably shorter than that

• Home compostable means that the final material can be fully composted into fertilizer without special conditions (heat, mechanical processing) in less than 90 days. See also: https://www.milieucentraal.nl/in-en-om-het-huis/tuinieren/t uinonderhoud/zelf-composteren/

• Materials are reusable when they can be reshaped without quality loss, requiring very little additional resources: for example by heating and recasting it, or by reversing a chemical reaction.

• If the ingredients used can largely be sourced as a by-product from something else, or from a waste stream the material qualifies for the tag: by-product or waste.

• We say a material is industrially compostable, when it can be broken down in an industrial composting facility, returning nutrients to the soil without leaving harmful residues.

• And lastly: when ingredients used are locally sourced and produced (locally or in neighbouring countries, or within 500 km), we can say its locally sourced.

What do the post processing tags mean?

• Make it yourself: The material is produced and shaped by hand using accessible tools and methods, without specialized industrial equipment. This includes hand-forming, simple casting in improvised molds, weaving, coating, and other craft-based approaches. Results vary with maker skill and material behavior, making it well-suited for experimentation and iteration.

• Molded / Casted: A fluid, paste-like, or otherwise shapeless material is poured or pressed into a mould and left to set. The mould defines the final form and can also impart surface texture. Typical applications include casting bioplastic sheets, filling slurry into shaped forms, and producing tiles or panels from composite mixtures.

• 3D Printed: Material is deposited or consolidated layer by layer to build up a three-dimensional form from a digital file. Depending on the material and printer type, this can involve extrusion, binding, or photocuring. Enables complex geometries and low-volume production with minimal tooling.

• Laser Cut: A focused laser beam cuts, scores, or engraves a flat sheet or plate according to a digital pattern. The process removes material with high precision, allowing intricate outlines and surface detailing. Particularly suited to sheet materials such as dried bioplastics, compressed fibers, and leather-like biocomposites. sheet or plate according to a digital pattern. • Laser Cut: A focused laser beam cuts, scores, or engraves a flat sheet or plate according to a digital pattern. The process removes material with high precision, allowing intricate outlines and surface detailing. Particularly suited to sheet materials such as dried bioplastics, compressed fibres, and leather-like biocomposites.

• Extruded: Material is forced through a shaped die or nozzle under pressure, producing a continuous profile — such as a rod, tube, sheet, or filament — that takes on the cross-section of the opening. The process can be applied to thermoplastic biomaterials, paste-like composites, and dough-like masses. Extrusion is also the basis of fused-deposition 3D printing.

• Hot Pressed: A solid material is heated until it becomes malleable, then pressed into or between moulds under controlled pressure. The material retains its mass and coherence throughout; only its shape changes. Once cooled, it holds the new form. Friction can substitute for external heat in some variants of the process.

• Cold Pressed: A solid material is shaped by pressing it into or between moulds at room temperature, without the addition of heat. Pressure alone causes the material to conform to the mould geometry. Suitable for materials that are sufficiently pliable or compressible without needing to be softened first.

• Vacuum Formed: A sheet material is heated until pliable, then drawn tightly over a mould by evacuating the air between sheet and mould surface. The resulting negative pressure holds the material against all contours of the mould as it cools and stiffens. The process works well for thin sheets and produces consistent surface detail.

• Assembled: Separate material parts are joined together so they can function as intended. Techniques include sewing, bonding with adhesives or heat, folding, heat sealing, and joinery methods such as snap-fit connections, interlocking geometries, and screws. The choice of joining method affects both the structural integrity and the disassembly or end-of-life potential of the result.

• Treated Surface: A treatment applied to a material surface for protection, decoration, or both — or a biomaterial developed specifically as a finish for another substrate. Techniques include painting, coating, varnishing, sanding, polishing, and print-based methods such as silkscreen, stencilling, UV-printing, and risography.

What are the different Open Hardware Licences and which one should I choose?

The materials library builds upon the open-source tradition: we learn from each other and share our insights so we can all develop further. You probably looked up recipes from other makers and created your own twist. But publishing your recipe doesn't mean it's no longer yours — you can decide how others use it by assigning an open source license to it.
Creative Commons licences protect the document you wrote — the specific words and layout of your recipe. If someone rewrites your instructions slightly, the licence no longer applies, and they can do whatever they want with it, including commercializing it without credit. The recipe knowledge itself — the ingredients, ratios, and process — is unprotected.
CERN Open Hardware licences protect the design — the knowledge embedded in the recipe itself, regardless of how it's written down. This is a much better fit for material recipes, because the value is in the making, not the prose.
CERN-OHL-S — Strongly reciprocal Everything derived from your recipe must be shared under the same licence. If someone improves your recipe, they have to publish that improvement openly too. Example: you develop a mycelium composite recipe and want to ensure every iteration of it stays in the commons.
